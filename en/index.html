ABOUT

Who Was Shinran?

Shinran Shonin (1173–1263) was a Buddhist monk of Japan's Kamakura period and is revered as the founder of Jodo Shinshu, the school of Pure Land Buddhism known in English as Shin Buddhism. Ordained at the age of nine, he spent twenty years in rigorous training on Mount Hiei, the great monastic center of his day. Yet he came to feel that the path of attaining enlightenment through his own efforts had reached a dead end, and at twenty-nine he left the mountain.

His decisive encounter was with the master Honen, through whom he entrusted himself to the teaching of the exclusive nembutsu: simply saying the Name of Amida Buddha and being saved by the Buddha's Vow. Even exile ― a persecution he suffered for this teaching ― became an occasion to spread the nembutsu among the ordinary people of eastern Japan. Through a life of ninety years, he clarified a path by which ordinary, foolish beings are saved just as they are.

Though a monk, Shinran openly married and raised a family, calling himself "neither monk nor layman." His way of life itself demonstrated that the Buddha's salvation is not reserved for special practitioners, but is open to every person living an everyday life.

WHY LIVE?

Why Live?

Why live? Why do we live at all — and why go on living when life feels heavy, meaningless, or unfair? This is one of the oldest human questions, and it was Shinran's own. He gave twenty years of relentless effort on Mount Hiei to answering it through discipline and self-perfection, and what he found was not an answer but a wall: the harder he tried to make himself worthy, the more clearly he saw that he could not.

The answer that finally reached him turns the question around. In Shin Buddhism, life is not a test you must pass, nor an achievement whose worth you have to prove. Your life is not a possession that needs justifying; it is something given — carried, moment by moment, by countless conditions that are not of your own making. Japanese Shin Buddhists express this in the phrase ikasarete ikiru: we do not simply live; we are being lived.

This does not make suffering disappear, and Shinran never pretended otherwise — he knew exile, hunger, and doubt. What his teaching says is more radical: this life, exactly as it is — imperfect, struggling, unable to fix itself — is already embraced by Amida Buddha's Vow. You do not have to become someone else before your life can matter. It matters now, as it is. And when that is received, saying the nembutsu, living itself changes character: it stops being a debt to repay and becomes a gift to respond to — with gratitude.

If you are asking "why live?" in a moment of deep distress, or are thinking of harming yourself, please reach out to someone now — a person you trust, or a crisis line in your country (you can find one at findahelpline.com). A page like this one is no substitute for a human voice.

TEACHINGS

Core Teachings

Shinran's teaching is a path by which "the self that can do nothing through its own power" is saved by the working of Amida Buddha's great Vow.

Other Power

TARIKI HONGAN 他力本願

"Other Power" does not mean relying on other people. It means the working of Amida Buddha's Primal Vow. We are saved not through our own calculations or efforts, but through the power of the Vow directed to us from the Buddha's side ― the very foundation of the teaching.

The Evil Person as the True Object

AKUNIN SHOKI 悪人正機

The "evil person" ― one incapable of doing good through their own power ― is precisely the person Amida's salvation is aimed at. The Vow works first of all for those who have awakened to their own limitations and the depth of their imperfection.

Nembutsu

NENBUTSU 念仏

Saying "Namu Amida Butsu" is not a condition or a practice performed in order to be saved. Shinran received it as the natural expression of gratitude toward the Buddha who is already at work to save us.

Neither Monk nor Layman

HISO HIZOKU 非僧非俗

Stripped of his clerical status by the exile, Shinran declared himself "neither monk nor layman" and took the name Gutoku ("foolish, stubble-haired") Shinran. Living as a householder ― eating meat, married ― while living in the nembutsu, he embodied a Buddhist path open to all.

WORDS

His Words

The Tannisho ("A Record in Lament of Divergences"), compiled by his disciple Yuien, preserves many sayings that convey Shinran's living voice.

Even a good person attains birth in the Pure Land ― how much more so an evil person.
― Tannisho, Chapter 3

This saying overturns common sense. Condensed within it is the teaching of akunin shoki: the true object of the Primal Vow is not the person who still relies on their own goodness, but the one who has nothing to depend on except the Buddha.

I, Shinran, do not have even a single disciple.
― Tannisho, Chapter 6

Those who say the nembutsu do so through the working of Amida Buddha ― they are not "my" disciples. These words convey Shinran's stance of walking alongside people as equals, as fellow travelers and companions who live together within the Vow.

The nembutsu alone is true and real.
― Tannisho, Epilogue

In this world of burning passions, where all things are empty and vain, the one thing that is not falsehood is the nembutsu. These words arose from Shinran's unflinching gaze into the uncertainty of all human endeavors ― and into his own heart.

LIFE

His Life

1173Birth

Born in Hino, on the outskirts of Kyoto, as a son of Hino Arinori, a court noble of modest rank.

1181Age 9

Ordained at Shoren-in temple. He then spends some twenty years in training on Mount Hiei.

1201Age 29

Descends Mount Hiei. After a hundred-day retreat at the Rokkakudo temple, he joins Honen's community and entrusts himself to the exclusive practice of the nembutsu.

1207Age 35

In the suppression of the nembutsu movement (the Jogen persecution), he is exiled to Echigo (present-day Niigata). Stripped of his clerical status, he takes his stand as "neither monk nor layman."

c. 1214Age 42

After being pardoned, he moves to the Kanto region (Hitachi province and elsewhere), where for some twenty years he shares the nembutsu teaching with farmers and ordinary people.

c. 1224Age 52

Drafts his major work, the Kyogyoshinsho. Jodo Shinshu regards this year as the founding of the school.

1230sAround age 60

Returns to Kyoto and devotes himself to writing ― completing the Kyogyoshinsho, composing hymns (wasan), and continuing to write into his final years.

1263Age 90

Passes away at the residence of his younger brother Jin'u (the Zenbo-bo temple quarters), fulfilling his lifelong wish for birth in the Pure Land.

WORKS

Major Writings

Kyogyoshinsho 教行信証

Shinran's magnum opus, formally titled "The True Teaching, Practice, and Realization of the Pure Land Way." Through extensive quotations from sutras and commentaries, it sets forth the doctrinal foundation of Jodo Shinshu.

The Three Books of Hymns 三帖和讃

A collective name for the Jodo Wasan, Koso Wasan, and Shozomatsu Wasan ― hymns in Japanese verse praising the virtues of the Buddhas and the teachings of the seven Pure Land masters, composed so that ordinary people could chant them together.

Shoshinge 正信偈

The "Hymn of True Entrusting," a verse concluding the Practice chapter of the Kyogyoshinsho. It praises Amida's Primal Vow and the lineage of the seven masters, and is still widely recited in morning and evening services today.

Tannisho 歎異抄

A record of Shinran's sayings believed to have been compiled after his death by his disciple Yuien. Vividly conveying Shinran's own words, it has moved countless readers through the centuries and remains one of the most beloved texts of Japanese Buddhism.
